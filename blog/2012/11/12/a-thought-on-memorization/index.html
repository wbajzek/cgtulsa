A Thought on Memorization

Nov 12th, 2012

I went through a phase for a few years where I felt like I couldn’t trust my musical memory. I had a small disaster while performing; a moment of distraction and the Courante from Bach’s third cello suite slipped from my mind. Unfortunately, I happened to be playing the Courante from Bach’s third cello suite at the time (I had the music in front of me, so it wasn’t a total tragedy). From that point on, though, I had lost my trust in my memory and, sure enough, lost my ability to memorize music.

A few years later, I got tired of this and decided to just start memorizing again. And I sort of could, but had no real insight as to why right away. More recently, my wife and I were working on Julian Bream’s arrangement for guitar and harpsichord of Boccherini’s Introduction and Fandango, and since it’s considered more ‘acceptable’ to use the music while playing in ensembles, I was using the music. I made no effort to memorize the piece, but quite quickly, I had done so, but it took me a while to understand why.

This piece had a lot of parts which were technically tricky for me. Many of Bream’s fingerings are awkward (although they made good sense musically), or required large, split-second shifts, or tremolo at a speeds faster than a solo tremolo piece would normally be played. In other words, it required me to spend more time examining what I was doing in greater detail than perhaps a piece would that I could easily read through at tempo. On top of this, regular duo rehearsals ensured that I still practiced the continuity of the piece.

I’ve recently begun learning a transcription of Albeniz’s Mallorca, which also requires careful planning of shifts, hand positions, timing, and dynamics. After two, maybe three practice sessions, I find that I already have many parts of it memorized already, despite not trying to actively memorize. I’m starting to think that as long as you give the music full attention to detail, memorization will follow naturally.
